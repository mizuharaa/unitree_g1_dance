 1. THE BIG IDEA — a dance video in, a balanced robot performance out
===============================================================================
Everything here serves one sentence:
  reference dance video  ->  the Unitree G1 performs that choreography,
                             reliably, without falling.

The end goal isn't a lab demo — it's a PLUG-AND-PLAY PRODUCT. An operator powers
the robot on, picks a dance from a library, hits deploy, and the robot performs
at paid-service quality, venue after venue. The primary target is LONG dances
(2-3 minutes), not 15-second clips.

Two things make it hard — keep them separate in your head:
  * GETTING THE MOVES — turning a video of a human into instructions a robot
    body can follow. THIS PART WORKS.
  * STAYING UP WHILE DOING THEM — performing those moves on two feet without
    toppling, even with real-world imperfections (delays, shoves, sensor noise).
    THIS is where the project currently gets stuck.

PLAIN VERSION: copying the dance is the easy half. Not falling over while
dancing is the hard half — because a humanoid is basically a tall stack of
motors trying to balance a broomstick that happens to be shaped like a person.


===============================================================================
 2. THE ROBOT — and why balance is the whole game
===============================================================================
A HUMANOID ROBOT is a robot shaped like a person (torso, two arms, two legs,
head). Ours is a Unitree G1 EDU Ultimate.

JOINTS, DoF, ACTUATORS, TORQUE
  * JOINT — a powered hinge (hip, knee, ankle, shoulder, elbow...). Our G1 has
    29 independently controllable joints.
  * DEGREE OF FREEDOM (DoF) — one independent way it can move. So this is a
    "29-DoF" robot, plus dexterous Inspire hands on the ends.
  * ACTUATOR — the motor + gearbox that drives a joint.
  * TORQUE — the twisting force a motor makes, in newton-metres (Nm). KEY FACT
    for later: every motor has a MAX torque. Ask for more and it SATURATES — it's
    already flooring it and can't push harder. A saturated ankle can't catch a
    wobble. That's the wall this project is hitting.

THE BALANCE PROBLEM
A humanoid is STATICALLY UNSTABLE — left alone it falls, like a pencil balanced
on a fingertip. It stays up only because a controller makes tiny corrections
many times a second. Two ideas describe "staying up":
  * CENTER OF MASS (CoM) — keep the robot's weight balanced over its feet.
  * STATION-KEEPING — stay on one spot instead of sliding. How far it slides is
    DRIFT, which we cap because the stage is only ~2 m across.

PLAIN VERSION: 29 little motors, each with a strength limit, cooperating 50
times a second to keep a person-shaped tower from tipping over — WHILE also
throwing dance shapes. When a move needs more ankle strength than the motor
has, it tips.


===============================================================================
 3. THE PIPELINE — from a phone video to a robot brain, in six stages
===============================================================================
The core of the software is a PIPELINE — an assembly line where each stage takes
the previous stage's output and transforms it.

  video --> [1 pose est.] --> 3D human --> [2 retarget] --> robot poses
        --> [3 clean/vet] --> motion file --> [4 train (RL, GPU)] --> policy.onnx
        --> [5 verify: the gate] --> pass? --> [6 deploy on robot]

STAGE 1 — SEE THE HUMAN (pose estimation)
  in: dance video   out: a moving 3D skeleton of the person
  POSE ESTIMATION is the computer-vision step that works out where the dancer's
  body was in 3D, frame by frame. We use a model called GVHMR. Its output is in
  a standard digital-human format called SMPL (a rigged 3D body described by a
  few numbers per frame). KEYPOINTS = the tracked points (wrists, ankles, hips)
  we later compare the robot against.

STAGE 2 — TRANSLATE TO THE ROBOT'S BODY (retargeting)
  in: 3D human motion   out: 29 robot joint angles per frame
  A human and the G1 have different proportions/joints, so you can't copy angles
  directly. RETARGETING maps the human motion onto the robot's skeleton. We use
  a library called GMR (General Motion Retargeting). Out comes a table of 29
  joint angles per frame, saved as CSV, then a compact binary motion file (.npz).

STAGE 3 — SANITY-CHECK THE MOVES (cleanup & vetting)
  in: raw robot motion   out: a clean, physically-possible motion
  Vision output is jittery, and some human moves are impossible for the robot
  (too fast, feet leave the 2 m circle, joint spins faster than the motor can).
  This stage FILTERS jitter (smoothing single-frame "twitches") and VETS
  FEASIBILITY (checks joint speeds vs limits, rejects the impossible). Terms:
  JERK = twitchiness; FEASIBILITY = can the robot physically do it.

STAGE 4 — TEACH THE ROBOT TO PERFORM IT (training)
  in: clean motion file   out: a trained controller, policy.onnx
  We do NOT "play back" the motion like a puppet. We TRAIN a controller that
  watches the robot's state and figures out how to move the joints to MATCH the
  reference dance WHILE balancing. This happens in simulation on a GPU and
  produces a small neural network called a POLICY. (See section 4.)

STAGE 5 — JUDGE WHETHER IT'S GOOD ENOUGH (the gate)
  in: trained policy   out: PASS/FAIL + a scorecard
  Before a policy touches the real robot, it runs an automated exam called the
  GATE — thousands of simulated robots dancing under stress (delays, shoves,
  noise), scored on survival, accuracy, drift, and motor strain. (See section 5.)

STAGE 6 — RUN IT FOR REAL (deploy)
  in: a passing policy   out: the robot dancing on the floor
  The policy runs on the robot's onboard computer, reading sensors and commanding
  motors 50 times a second — with a human holding a safety remote. (See sec. 6.)


===============================================================================
 4. THE LEARNING PART — reinforcement learning (RL)
===============================================================================
The controller is trained with REINFORCEMENT LEARNING (RL). Plain loop:
  try something -> get a score -> adjust to score higher -> repeat millions of
  times.

VOCABULARY
  POLICY       The "brain." A neural net that, given what the robot senses,
               outputs the 29 joint commands. The thing we're training.
  OBSERVATION  What the policy sees each step: joint positions/speeds, body tilt,
               and where the reference dance says it should be. Ours = 160 numbers.
  ACTION       What the policy outputs: target angles for the 29 joints.
  REWARD       The score. High for matching the dance AND staying up AND not
               slamming the motors; low for drifting or falling. Shaping this is
               most of the "recipe" work.
  ENVIRONMENT  The simulated world where it practices.
  EPISODE      One practice attempt (e.g. one run of the 49 s dance). A fall ends
               it early.
  ITERATION    One round of "collect experience -> nudge the policy." We run
               ~10,000-12,000 per dance.

WHY SIMULATION, AND WHY A GPU?
You can't teach this on the real robot — it would fall thousands of times and
destroy itself. So we train in a PHYSICS SIMULATOR, running 4,096 virtual robots
in parallel, all learning together. That parallelism needs a GPU (a chip that
does thousands of calculations at once). Our dev laptop has NO GPU, so training
runs on a rented cloud GPU (an NVIDIA RTX 4090). This constraint drove several
big decisions (section 8).

DOMAIN RANDOMIZATION — the trick that makes sim transfer to reality
A policy trained in a PERFECT simulation fails in the real world, which is
messier. So during training we deliberately RANDOMIZE the sim: vary the robot's
weight, add sensor noise, inject control delays, shove the robot randomly. This
is DOMAIN RANDOMIZATION, and it's what lets a sim-trained brain survive on real
hardware. The gap it fights is the SIM-TO-REAL GAP.

The RL algorithm is PPO (a standard, stable method), run by a library called
rsl_rl, inside a training framework called mjlab. Why mjlab and not the more
famous Isaac Lab is the most-asked question — answered in section 8.

PLAIN VERSION: we breed a dance controller by having thousands of simulated
robots practise at once, scoring each on "did you match the dance AND stay up
AND go easy on the motors," and keeping what scores best — while roughing up the
simulation so the winner also works in the real, messy world.


===============================================================================
 5. JUDGING IT — the gate and its metrics
===============================================================================
A trained policy is NOT trusted just because training reward went up. It must
pass the GATE — an independent test that runs the policy on ~128 simulated robots
through the full dance under many CONDITIONS, and scores it.

  METRIC          WHAT IT MEASURES                                    BAR
  -----------     ------------------------------------------------    ----------
  Survival        % of simulated robots that finish without falling   >= 99%
  Drift (m)       How far it slid from where it should stand          <= 1.0 m
  mpkpe (m)       Mean per-keypoint position error: how closely the
                  body matches the reference, in world space          low
  rr_mpkpe        Same, ignoring WHERE it stands: pure pose accuracy.
                  low rr + high mpkpe = "right poses, sliding away"    <= 0.1 m
  Ankle p95 (Nm)  Near-peak ankle torque. Hitting the 50 Nm limit =
                  saturation = about to fall                          <= 15 / 20
  Latency         Injected control delay (20/40/60/80 ms) to mimic
                  real electronics                                    survive 40ms
  Push            Random shoves mid-dance (balance robustness)        survive

The gate passes ONLY if EVERY check passes. The realistic
"will-it-survive-the-robot" condition is 40ms delay + push + noise — that's the
one we key on. HELD-OUT EVALUATION = a second check on UNSEEN random conditions,
so we know the policy generalizes rather than got lucky.

TWO KINDS OF VERIFICATION
  * SIM-TO-SIM — re-test in a DIFFERENT simulator to catch overfitting to the
    training sim. (We tried; it hit a snag — see war stories.)
  * ROBOT DAY — the real, final test on the physical robot, always on a GANTRY
    (safety frame) first, with a human on the kill-switch.


===============================================================================
 6. DEPLOYMENT — getting a policy onto the real robot, safely
===============================================================================
Once a policy passes the gate, it runs on the robot's onboard computer — an
NVIDIA Jetson Orin ("PC2"). The CONTROL LOOP runs at 50 Hz (50x/second):
  read sensors -> policy computes joint targets -> send to motors -> repeat.
Any slower or jittery and the robot wobbles, which is why we hardened the loop.

HOW JOINT TARGETS BECOME MOTION — PD CONTROL & GAINS
The policy outputs TARGET angles, not raw power. A lower-level controller called
PD CONTROL turns "go to this angle" into actual torque, using tuning numbers
called GAINS (stiffness kp and damping kd). These must match what training
assumed, or a good policy behaves badly on hardware.

SAFETY — NOT OPTIONAL
  *** This tetherless G1 has NO torque-cutting hardware e-stop. ***
The only real stops are the remote's B-damping button (motors go soft) and the
power switch. So every deploy requires a HUMAN PHYSICALLY PRESENT with the
damping remote, the robot on a gantry for first runs, and a typed confirmation
in the app. The safety review baked these in as hard, non-skippable gates.

SNEAKY SIM-TO-REAL GAPS
Some things are true in sim but not measurable on the real robot — e.g. the
robot's exact sideways velocity (base_lin_vel) is an INPUT the policy expects
but hardware can't directly sense; it needs a STATE ESTIMATOR to reconstruct it.
Mismatches like this are the essence of the sim-to-real gap.

 7. THE TECH STACK & THE OPERATOR APP
===============================================================================
The whole stack, by what each piece does (nothing exotic — the skill is in how
they're wired together and hardened):

  LAYER               TOOLS                                     ROLE
  ----------------    --------------------------------------    -------------------------------
  Perception          GVHMR, SMPL / SMPL-X                      Video -> 3D human motion
  Retargeting         GMR                                       Human -> 29-DoF robot motion
  Motion prep         custom Python, NumPy/SciPy                De-jitter, feasibility, ramps
  Training            mjlab 1.5.0, rsl_rl (PPO), MuJoCo+Warp    RL controller training on GPU
  Simulation          MuJoCo, mujoco_menagerie, unitree_mujoco  Physics for train/sandbox/gate
  Verification        custom gate, held-out eval, sim sandbox   Prove a policy before hardware
  Policy format       ONNX                                      Portable trained-net file
  Deploy runtime      unitree_sdk2_python, CycloneDDS, onnxrt   50 Hz control loop on the Jetson
  Cloud compute       GreenNode (RTX 4090 notebooks)            Rented GPU for train/extract
  Experiment tracking Weights & Biases (W&B)                    Logs training curves & artifacts
  App backend         FastAPI (Python)                          Local engine + API on :8735
  App frontend        React, Vite, Tailwind/shadcn              Operator console UI
  Desktop shell       pywebview (PySide6/Qt)                    Wraps the web UI as a desktop app

THE APP IS AN "OPERATOR CONSOLE," NOT A DASHBOARD
Because the end goal is a product an operator runs at a show, the app ("G1 Dance
Studio") is built like mission-control. Already built:
  * Five-stage pipeline view with drag-and-drop, logs, blockers, retry, and a
    training-approval step.
  * Live run state machine + estimated dance timeline; a big global STOP button.
  * SHOW MODE: dance library, per-venue records, setlist builder, perform mode.
  * Compute/cost/training monitor (live GPU %, spend, iterations) so you don't
    have to SSH to check.
  * Held-out stats per dance + one-click POLICY ROLLBACK to a previous version.
  * Safety UX: a typed confirmation phrase, fall/incident capture, an immutable
    audit timeline.
  * Signed verification verdicts (tamper-proof), music/audio sync, and a
    side-by-side "dance studio" preview (reference vs. what the policy does).

IN PROGRESS / NEXT: the "motion surgery" approach to the survival problem
(section 10), best-checkpoint selection (just added), and — pending a hardware
repair — real robot-day validation.


===============================================================================
 8. KEY DECISIONS — including "WHY NOT ISAAC LAB?"
===============================================================================
[THE BIG ONE] Why mjlab, not Isaac Lab / the prebuilt BeyondMimic?
  The research method we're copying is BeyondMimic, built on ISAAC LAB — NVIDIA's
  RL robotics framework, which runs on ISAAC SIM, a large Omniverse-based
  simulator. The catch is our rented cloud GPU: GreenNode gives us a FIXED
  software image (PyTorch 2.5.1 / CUDA 12.4) with NO Docker and no way to install
  the heavyweight, driver-specific Omniverse/Isaac Sim stack. So Isaac Lab simply
  would NOT run there ("permanently dead on this image").
  The fix: MJLAB — a newer, lighter library that reimplements the SAME
  motion-tracking RL approach on MuJoCo + Warp (GPU physics) instead of Isaac Sim.
  It installs with plain pip, ships its own motion converter (no Isaac Sim needed),
  has built-in ONNX export and push randomization, and runs on the fixed image.
  It was pre-planned as a "bounded fallback" — and the fallback became the main road.

[FOLLOW-UP] "So why write custom code at all, if the libraries exist?"
  We DO lean on prebuilt libraries heavily (mjlab, rsl_rl, GMR, GVHMR, MuJoCo).
  But the stock library gets you a RESEARCH DEMO — a policy that tracks a dance in
  a clean simulation. Turning that into something that survives on REAL HARDWARE —
  robust to latency, shoves, motor-torque limits, and floor drift — is the custom
  part. That's what our sim2real_task_v5/v6/v7 "recipes" are: extra reward terms,
  termination rules, and training curricula layered on top of mjlab's stock task.
  The acceptance GATE and the operator APP are also ours.
  Rule of thumb: libraries give you "it dances in sim"; the custom code is
  everything between there and "it dances on the robot, at a show, safely."

OTHER DECISIONS WORTH KNOWING
  * CLOUD GPU, DELETED NOT PAUSED. GreenNode bills from instance CREATION to
    DELETION — pausing still charges. Workflow: spin up, train, pull results,
    DELETE. (We've twice been burned by a box idling after a run — watch this.)
  * HELD-OUT mjlab VERIFICATION instead of true sim-to-sim. The plan was to verify
    in a second simulator; its robot model wasn't dynamically equivalent to
    mjlab's (it even fell holding a STATIC pose), so a cross-sim test gives false
    failures. We switched to testing in the same faithful sim with unseen
    conditions + strong randomization, and made ROBOT DAY the true independent test.
  * DESKTOP APP, NOT A WEBSITE. An operator at a venue shouldn't depend on a
    browser tab or a server — the UI is wrapped as a native desktop window
    (all-Python, via pywebview).
  * LONG DANCES NEED IN-PLACE CHOREOGRAPHY. Stock motion-capture dances travel too
    far for a 2 m stage; real show dances must have a small footprint.


===============================================================================
 9. BUGS & WAR STORIES — mistakes and what they taught us
===============================================================================
* SILENT VERSION DRIFT BROKE THE GPU PHYSICS. A loose install pulled slightly
  newer physics libs (mujoco-warp, warp-lang) that crashed with a cryptic CUDA
  error at startup. Fix: PIN EXACT VERSIONS. In GPU/ML stacks, "latest" is a
  liability — lock everything.

* A RENDERING FLAG CORRUPTED TRAINING. Setting MUJOCO_GL=egl (needed for
  RENDERING) during TRAINING collided with the GPU physics engine ("illegal
  memory access"). Fix: rendering flag on only for the verify step.

* CHECKPOINTS SORTED ALPHABETICALLY. Code picked the "latest" saved policy by
  NAME, so model_500 sorted after model_3999 — we resumed from the wrong, earlier
  checkpoint and lost a whole training stage. Fix: sort NUMERICALLY.

* "num_envs DEFAULTS TO 1." The trainer silently defaults to ONE simulated robot
  unless you pass 4,096 explicitly — easy to run a 4,000x slower job unnoticed.

* A MIS-READ SENSOR INDEX DROVE A DAY OF WRONG CONCLUSIONS. One mis-indexed number
  in a sim readout led to confident-but-wrong diagnoses for a full day. Project
  rule now: never call a finding decisive without an independent cross-check, and
  commit the measurement script + its raw output so numbers have provenance.

* ...AND A FRESH ONE THIS WEEK. While diagnosing a "the motion is 16x too fast!"
  scare, the alarming number came from a one-character bug in the ANALYSIS code
  (np.diff(x, 0) — which diffs zero times — instead of axis=0). The motion was
  fine. Caught before spending a cent of GPU.

* A GOOD SAVE: BEST-CHECKPOINT SELECTION. Late in one run the policy COLLAPSED to
  3% survival in its final steps. We used to export the LAST checkpoint
  automatically — which would've shipped the collapse. The new step screens the
  last several checkpoints and exports the best one. It immediately earned its keep.


===============================================================================
 10. WHERE WE ARE RIGHT NOW
===============================================================================
THE SURVIVAL WALL (software)
After four training attempts, one number won't move: NOMINAL SURVIVAL is stuck at
~86-92% (we need >= 99%). Each recipe change improves one metric and dents
another — we recently SOLVED drift (1.67 m -> 0.81 m) but survival got slightly
worse. Crucially, the falls always land in the SAME TWO MOMENTS of the dance
(~13-18 s and ~25-36 s), and at exactly those moments the ANKLE MOTORS SATURATE —
they hit their 50 Nm limit.

  THE REALIZATION: this is NOT a reward-tuning problem anymore. At those two beats
  the choreography demands a faster weight-shift than the ankle motors can
  physically deliver, so the robot runs out of strength and tips. You cannot add
  torque with a reward weight. The next move is "MOTION SURGERY" — gently
  slowing/softening only those two beats so the ankle demand drops under the limit
  — validated on the laptop before spending any more GPU money.

THE BURNT DC-DC CONVERTER (hardware)
Separately, the robot is currently DOWN: a foul burnt-electronics smell traced to
a DC-DC CONVERTER in the torso — the circuitry that steps the ~48 V battery down
to the low-voltage rails that run the computer. It's powered off, battery
disconnected, awaiting a support RMA. Good news: this is INDEPENDENT of the
software — training continues in the cloud, so no time is lost during the repair.

WHERE THAT LEAVES US: the pipeline works and the app is mature; the controller is
close but not yet deploy-grade because of a genuine physical limit at two dance
beats; and the robot needs a power-board repair before the next real-world test.
Two clear, separate tracks — one software, one hardware.


===============================================================================
 11. GLOSSARY — quick reference
===============================================================================
DoF (degree of freedom)  One independent way a robot can move. Our G1 has 29.
Actuator                 A joint's motor + gearbox. Produces torque, has a limit.
Torque (Nm)              Rotational force. Every motor has a max; over it =
                         saturation.
Saturation               A motor at full strength, unable to push harder. Cause
                         of the current falls.
Pose estimation          Inferring a 3D body pose from ordinary video (GVHMR).
SMPL                     A standard digital-human body format; pose-est output.
Retargeting              Mapping human motion onto the robot's skeleton (GMR).
Policy                   The trained neural-net controller — the robot's "brain."
Reinforcement learning   Learning by trial, reward, and adjustment, over millions
  (RL)                   of tries.
Reward                   The training score: match the dance + stay up + spare
                         the motors.
Episode                  One practice attempt (e.g. one dance run); a fall ends it.
Domain randomization     Roughing up the sim (noise, delays, shoves) so the policy
                         survives reality.
Sim-to-real gap          The difference between simulation and the real robot —
                         the core challenge.
The gate                 The automated exam a policy must pass before hardware.
Survival                 % of simulated robots that finish without falling.
Drift (m)                How far the robot slides from its intended spot.
mpkpe / rr_mpkpe         How accurately the body matches the reference (world /
                         pose-only).
PD control & gains       The low-level rule (stiffness/damping knobs) that drives
                         a joint to a target angle.
Control loop (50 Hz)     Sense -> decide -> command, 50x/second, on the Jetson.
ONNX                     A portable file format for the trained policy, at deploy.
mjlab                    The MuJoCo-based RL framework we train in (Isaac Lab
                         replacement).
Gantry                   A safety frame that holds the robot up during first
                         real-world tests.
